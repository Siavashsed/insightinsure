## QUORA
**Answer this question:** How did the FCA's 2021 price walking ban change home and motor insurance for UK consumers?

The FCA's price walking ban, which took effect in January 2022, was designed to address a significant market failure: insurers were routinely charging loyal, long-standing customers more at renewal than they charged new customers for identical or near-identical coverage. This practice had gone largely unchecked for years.

The ban's core requirement is straightforward—insurers can no longer charge existing customers a higher price than new customers for equivalent policies. This sounds simple, but its impact has been nuanced.

For consumers, the immediate benefit is theoretical price protection. Loyalty is no longer punished in the crude way it was before. However, I've observed (and detailed in further analysis here: https://siavashsed.github.io/insightinsure/fca-2021-price-walking-ban-insurance/) that the market has adapted.

What actually changed:

1. Direct loyalty penalties largely disappeared
2. Renewal quotes became more competitive by law
3. However, subtle gaps persist through coverage adjustments, excess modifications, or bundling tactics

The real shift is behavioral, not financial. Millions of policyholders—especially older, less digitally engaged customers—still don't actively shop around at renewal, allowing insurers to maintain profitable pricing through inertia.

The ban works best for informed consumers who compare quotes. For passive renewals, the protection is weaker than many assume. Your best defense remains annual shopping around.
