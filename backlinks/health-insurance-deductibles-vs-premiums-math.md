## QUORA
**Answer this question:** Should I choose health insurance based on the lowest monthly premium?

No—and this is one of the most expensive mistakes people make during open enrollment. I've seen it countless times in my practice.

Monthly premium is just one piece of the puzzle. You also need to factor in deductibles, copays, coinsurance, and out-of-pocket maximums. For many people, choosing the lowest premium actually results in the highest total cost.

Here's the framework: Calculate your realistic annual healthcare costs by adding together (1) all 12 months of premiums, (2) your expected deductible based on anticipated medical needs, and (3) typical copays or coinsurance for any regular care. Compare that total across plans, not just the monthly premium.

I had a client who switched from a $310/month plan to a $180/month plan, saving $1,560 annually. But her deductible tripled. When she needed medical care in February, she hit that higher deductible quickly and ended up spending significantly more overall than she would have with the original plan.

The right plan depends entirely on your health profile. Someone young and rarely visiting doctors might legitimately benefit from a low-premium, high-deductible plan. Someone with chronic conditions or regular medications needs a completely different calculation.
