How Insurance Companies Calculate Your Premium (And What You Can Actually Do About It)

Insurance broker Patrick Lane explains exactly how insurers price your premium and which moves genuinely lower it. Plain language, no jargon.

By   Patrick Lane Filed   Insurance Advice & Comparisons Read   6 min

Your insurer isn't guessing when they send you that renewal quote. They're running you through a scoring model built from millions of data points, and most people have no idea what's feeding it. I had a client in Bristol last year, a retired teacher named Margaret, who was paying £1,340 a year on her home insurance. Same house, same contents, same claims history as three years prior. We shopped it together one Tuesday afternoon and had her down to £810 within an hour. Not because she'd done anything wrong before. Because she'd never been told how the pricing actually works.

Insurance pricing looks like a black box from the outside. But it's not random. Insurers use a method called actuarial rating, which essentially means they sort you into risk groups and charge you based on how expensive people like you have historically been to insure. The problem is 'people like you' covers a lot of ground you've never consented to. Your postcode, your job title, your credit score in many markets, the age of your roof, your overnight parking arrangement. All of it feeds the model. And the model is designed to make the insurer money, not to give you a fair price.

The Core Factors Insurers Actually Use to Price You

Every insurer weights these differently, but the building blocks are pretty consistent across the market. For car insurance, they're looking at your age, driving history, vehicle make and model, annual mileage, where the car is kept overnight, and increasingly, your credit history. For home insurance, it's the rebuild cost of the property, your postcode's flood and subsidence risk, the type of construction, and your claims history going back typically five years. For life insurance, it's your age, health, smoking status, and family medical history.

What most people miss is that the relationship between these factors isn't linear. A 25-year-old with one speeding conviction and a Ford Focus doesn't pay slightly more than a 25-year-old with a clean record. They might pay 40% more. The risk multipliers compound. And here's the thing... insurers are also looking at behavioural signals you'd never think of. Paying monthly rather than annually, for instance, often gets read as a mild credit risk indicator. The Association of British Insurers acknowledges that pricing models have become significantly more granular over the past decade as insurers gained access to richer data sources.

Credit-based insurance scoring is worth understanding, particularly if you're in the US market. A 2007 Federal Trade Commission report found that credit-based insurance scores are effective predictors of risk but also tend to produce higher premiums for lower-income and minority groups, raising serious fairness questions the industry still hasn't fully answered.

The Loyalty Penalty Is Real and Documented

This is where I get genuinely annoyed on behalf of consumers. Insurers have spent years rewarding new customers with competitive rates while quietly inflating renewal premiums for existing policyholders. It has a name in the industry: price walking. And it worked because most people assume loyalty should be rewarded. It wasn't. It was exploited.

The FCA stepped in with a ban, and if you want to understand how significant that shift was, the full story is covered well in how the FCA's 2020 Price Walking Ban changed insurance comparisons forever. The short version: from January 2022 onwards, UK insurers were required to offer renewal prices no higher than the equivalent new customer price. But the regulation applies to the channel, not the market. A customer who's been with Insurer A for four years and never shopped around is still probably overpaying compared to what Insurer B would charge. The loyalty penalty between insurers never went away. Only the penalty within a single insurer was addressed.

So shopping around at renewal isn't optional. It's the single most effective thing most people can do. The FCA's general insurance pricing practices market study estimated that around 6 million policyholders in the UK were paying significantly elevated prices due to loyalty-related pricing before the reforms. Six million. That's not a niche problem.

Specific Moves That Actually Reduce Your Premium

Let me be direct about what works and what's mostly noise. These are the changes that consistently move the needle, in my experience working with clients across motor, home, and life products.

  • Increase your excess voluntarily. Raising your voluntary excess from £250 to £500 on a car policy can cut your premium by 10-15% in many cases. Just make sure you can actually afford to pay it if you claim.
  • Pay annually, not monthly. Monthly payments often come with interest or a loading. Paying upfront removes that cost entirely, typically saving somewhere between 10% and 20% on the total.
  • Correct your job title carefully. Insurers rate on occupation. 'Chef' and 'catering manager' can produce meaningfully different quotes for identical applicants. You can't lie, but you can be precise. Check how your insurer categorises roles.
  • Consider a telematics policy if you drive low mileage. Black box policies reward provably safe drivers. If you drive under around 7,000 miles a year and drive sensibly, this can cut motor premiums significantly for younger drivers especially.
  • Build your no-claims discount and protect it. A five-year no-claims bonus on motor insurance is a significant pricing asset. Protecting it costs a little but is almost always worth it for anyone with four or more years built up.

One thing that doesn't always work as advertised: multi-policy discounts. In theory, bundling home and motor with the same insurer should save you money. In practice, when I've tested this across comparison platforms, the bundled price is often uncompetitive on at least one of the products. You're better off getting the best price on each product separately and revisiting bundling only if the numbers actually stack up.

And a quick note on the broader regulatory landscape: the FCA's Consumer Duty, which came into force in 2023, imposes a genuine obligation on insurers to demonstrate fair value. If you want to understand what that means for you as a buyer, the detail is laid out clearly in what the FCA's Consumer Duty actually meant for insurance buyers. The practical upshot is that you now have a stronger basis for challenging a renewal price that looks disproportionate.

When the Market Itself Is the Problem

Here's something that doesn't get said enough: sometimes you can do everything right and still see your premium rise sharply. That's not because your risk profile changed. It's because the underlying insurance market for your product or region has hardened. Insurers have paid out more in claims than expected, reinsurance costs have jumped, or a category of risk has been repriced across the board. You're not being punished individually. The whole pool got more expensive.

This is most visible in home insurance in high-risk areas. The situation that played out in California is a stark example of what market failure looks like when climate risk outpaces the pricing models. The collapse of standard market availability and the strain placed on the insurer of last resort is something I'd encourage anyone in a high-risk area to read about carefully. The breakdown is documented in detail in what California's FAIR Plan crisis in 2023 really meant for homeowners. It matters because similar dynamics are beginning to appear in flood-prone areas of the UK and parts of coastal Europe.

Okay, I'll be honest for a second because I think about this a lot and I'm not sure I have a clean answer. My friend Dave tried the 'just cancel and come back as a new customer' trick with his broadband provider and it worked brilliantly. He then tried it with his home insurer and it was... a disaster, mostly because he didn't read the cooling-off period properly and ended up with a gap in cover over Christmas when a pipe burst. I've told that story to clients as a cautionary tale ever since. The point being: the tactics that work in other subscription markets don't always translate to insurance cleanly, and a gap in cover can cost you far more than you'd ever save on a year's premium. I'm not 100% sure the 'cancel and re-quote' approach is worth the risk for most people, even when the numbers look tempting.

The one thing I'd push back on hardest is the misconception that premium increases are always a signal to just accept and pay. They're not. They're a prompt to act. Which? has consistently found in its annual surveys that switching or renegotiating at renewal saves the average UK household meaningful money each year, often hundreds of pounds across motor and home combined. The market rewards action.

Before your next renewal lands, set a calendar reminder for three weeks before the expiry date. That's your window to shop properly, get at least three quotes from outside your current insurer, and call your existing insurer armed with the best alternative price you've found. Insurers have retention teams whose job is to match or beat competitor quotes. Most people never call. The ones who do almost always save money.
