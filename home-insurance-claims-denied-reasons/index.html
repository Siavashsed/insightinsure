Home Insurance Claims That Get Denied: The Real Reasons Insurers Say No

Home insurance claims get denied more often than you'd think. Independent broker Patrick Lane explains the real reasons - and how to fight back.

By   Patrick Lane Filed   Insurance Advice & Comparisons Read   5 min

Most people assume their home insurance will pay out when something goes wrong. It won't - not always, and sometimes not even close. I had a client in early 2022, a retired teacher in Warwickshire, who filed a claim after a burst pipe destroyed her kitchen floor and the cabinets beneath the sink. The insurer denied the whole thing. The reason buried in her policy? The pipe had been showing signs of slow corrosion for months, which they classified as a 'gradual deterioration' exclusion rather than sudden damage. She'd been paying premiums for eleven years without a single claim. That denial cost her around £4,200 out of pocket. My position is clear: far too many legitimate claims are declined because of policy language that was never properly explained at the point of sale, and consumers are paying the price for it.

This isn't a rare edge case. The Financial Ombudsman Service consistently records home insurance as one of the highest complaint categories in the UK, with tens of thousands of disputes raised each year over claim outcomes. A significant chunk of those involve denials that the ombudsman later overturns in the consumer's favour - which tells you something important about how those denials were handled in the first place. If you've just had a claim refused, or you want to know what to watch for before it happens, this is what you actually need to know.

The 'Gradual Damage' Trap That Catches Most People Off Guard

Almost every home insurance policy excludes damage that's happened slowly over time. Rot, damp, rust, subsidence that's been creeping for years - insurers call this 'wear and tear' or 'gradual deterioration,' and they use it to deny claims that look, on the surface, like sudden events. The burst pipe scenario I mentioned is a classic example. The final dramatic moment - water flooding your kitchen at 11pm on a Tuesday - feels sudden. But if there's any evidence the pipe was degrading beforehand, the insurer can argue the underlying cause wasn't sudden at all.

This is the single most common reason home insurance claims are denied in the UK. Insurers employ loss adjusters whose job, at least in part, is to identify whether damage could be attributed to a pre-existing condition. I've seen this pattern play out dozens of times in my work with clients, and the frustrating part is that homeowners genuinely didn't know the damage was developing. That doesn't matter to the claims handler reading a policy exclusion clause.

What you can do: keep a record of your home's condition. A dated photo of your pipes, roof, and gutters once a year costs you nothing and gives you evidence if the slow-damage argument ever gets used against you. It won't guarantee a payout, but it weakens the insurer's case considerably.

Underinsurance: Why the Number on Your Policy Probably Isn't Right

Here's a denial-adjacent problem that I'd argue is even more common than outright refusals: your claim gets accepted in principle, but the payout is slashed because your home was underinsured. The Association of British Insurers estimated in 2023 that around one in five homes in the UK is underinsured, often because rebuild costs have risen sharply since the policy was taken out. If your policy covers a rebuild value of £200,000 but the actual cost is £280,000, many insurers apply something called the 'average clause' - they pay only the proportionate amount of your claim. So a £40,000 claim becomes a £28,500 payout. That's not a denial in name, but it has the same financial impact.

The fix here is simple in theory and annoying in practice. You need to use a proper rebuild cost calculator, not just copy the market value of your property. The Building Cost Information Service (BCIS) runs a free online calculator specifically for this, and it takes about four minutes to use. Review your rebuild figure every year, especially right now when construction costs have been unusually volatile.

I'm also going to mention something the comparison sites won't tell you: the cheapest policy is often cheap because the rebuild limit is set unrealistically low. If you want to understand the real trade-off between what you pay now and what you get back later, the same logic I'd apply to health insurance deductibles versus premiums applies here - the maths of a low-premium policy only look good until you actually need to claim.

The Policy Conditions Nobody Reads Until It's Too Late

Okay, this is where I have to be honest about something slightly embarrassing. Years ago, before I'd fully learned my trade, I helped a friend - my mate Dave, who runs a small landscape gardening business out of Shropshire - arrange a home policy for his new property. He mentioned in passing that he sometimes stored work equipment in the garage. I nodded along and didn't flag that as a material fact worth disclosing. Fast forward eighteen months, his garage got broken into, tools worth about £3,500 were stolen, and the insurer denied the claim because the premises were being used for business purposes without disclosure. I felt awful. It was my oversight as much as his. I've never skipped that question with a client since.

Non-disclosure is a legitimate and very common reason for claim denial. UK insurers operate on the principle of 'utmost good faith,' meaning you're expected to volunteer relevant information even if they don't ask the exact right question. Running a home business, having a criminal record, owning certain dog breeds, letting out a room - all of these can void a policy or lead to a denied claim if they weren't disclosed upfront. The FCA's Consumer Duty rules introduced in 2022 did push insurers to ask clearer questions at the point of sale, which helps. But the obligation to disclose material facts still sits with you.

Read your policy's 'conditions precedent' section. These are actions you must take for your cover to remain valid - things like maintaining working locks, installing alarms you said you had, or notifying your insurer within a set number of days of an incident. Missing a conditions precedent requirement gives the insurer grounds to deny your claim even when the damage itself would normally be covered.

What to Do When Your Claim Gets Denied

Don't just accept the denial. That sounds obvious, but most people do. The Financial Ombudsman Service upholds roughly 30 to 40 percent of home insurance complaints in the consumer's favour in any given year, which means a meaningful proportion of denied claims are being wrongly refused. You have a formal complaints process available to you, and if the insurer doesn't resolve it to your satisfaction within eight weeks, you can escalate to the ombudsman for free.

When you challenge a denial, be specific. Don't just say you think the decision is unfair. Point to the exact policy wording they're relying on, ask them to explain precisely how your situation meets that exclusion, and request the loss adjuster's report if one was produced. The Consumer Duty framework now requires insurers to treat customers fairly and explain decisions clearly - use that language in your complaint letter. Insurers know an ombudsman referral is costly and time-consuming for them too, and a well-argued complaint often gets a second look before it gets that far.

One more thing worth knowing: the way your insurer priced your policy in the first place can shape how they handle claims. Since the FCA's 2020 crackdown on price walking, renewal pricing has become more transparent, but insurers still vary widely in how they treat customers at the claims stage. Looking up a company's ombudsman uphold rate before you buy is something most people never do... and it's one of the most useful pieces of information you can find. The FOS publishes complaints data by firm twice a year. Use it.

Today, before anything else goes wrong, pull out your home insurance policy and check two things: your declared rebuild value against the BCIS calculator, and your conditions precedent section for any obligations you might be failing to meet right now. Those two checks alone will put you in a far stronger position than the majority of homeowners who only read their policy the day after a claim is denied.
