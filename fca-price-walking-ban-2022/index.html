The FCA's Price Walking Ban: What Actually Changed for Insurance Buyers in 2022

The FCA's price walking ban took full effect in 2022. Here's what it really meant for insurance buyers, and whether it delivered on its promise.

By   Patrick Lane Filed   Insurance Advice & Comparisons Read   5 min

In January 2022, the FCA's ban on price walking finally kicked in across home and motor insurance, and it was arguably the biggest shift in retail insurance pricing in a generation. I remember sitting with a client in late 2021, a retired teacher from Bristol named Margaret, who had been renewing her home insurance with the same provider for eleven years. Her renewal quote that November was £487. I ran her details through a comparison tool and found the same insurer was offering new customers cover for £271. Same policy. Same address. Same insurer. That £216 gap was legal, normalized, and about to become illegal. The price walking ban was supposed to end exactly that. My argument is this: it worked better than the industry expected, but not quite as well as consumers deserved.

Price walking, for anyone who hasn't heard the term, is the practice of gradually raising renewal premiums year after year for existing customers, even when their risk profile hasn't changed, simply because loyalty tends to suppress shopping behaviour. The FCA spent years documenting this. Their final market study report found that around 6 million policyholders were paying significantly high prices in their markets, and that home and motor insurance customers combined were overpaying by roughly £1.2 billion a year. By January 2022, the new rules required that any renewal price offered to an existing customer could be no higher than the equivalent new customer price. Simple in theory. Complicated in practice.

What the Rules Actually Required Insurers to Do

The FCA's new pricing rules, which came into force on 1 January 2022, didn't just ban price walking in a vague, aspirational sense. They set a specific, enforceable standard: the renewal price for an existing customer must be no greater than the price that customer would be quoted as a new customer through the same channel. So if you came through a comparison site as a new customer, your renewal the following year had to match that equivalent price. No more loyalty penalty baked into the system.

Insurers also had to make it easier for customers to cancel auto-renewal, and they were required to tell customers who had been renewing for four or more consecutive years that shopping around might save them money. That last part mattered. In my work with first-time buyers and older clients especially, I've seen how rarely people actually read renewal letters, and how much a direct, plain-language prompt can change behaviour. The FCA was essentially mandating a nudge that brokers like me had been giving clients for years.

There were genuine compliance costs. Pricing systems that had been built over decades around differential renewal pricing needed overhauling. The Association of British Insurers flagged concerns that the transition could flatten pricing in ways that inadvertently increased premiums for genuinely low-risk new customers. That concern wasn't entirely wrong, as we'd see later in the year. For more on this, see The FCA's 2021 Price Walking Ban: What It Actually Changed for Insurance Buyers.

The First-Year Results: Promising, With Some Catches

The early data in 2022 was actually encouraging. The FCA's own review of pricing practices published in 2022 found that the number of home insurance customers paying what it called "very high" prices relative to their risk had dropped substantially in the first months of the new rules. That was real, measurable progress. Consumers who had been passively renewing were, in many cases, seeing lower bills without doing anything differently.

But here's the thing... it didn't land equally for everyone. Younger drivers and new homeowners who had previously benefited from sharp introductory offers found that some of those offers got less aggressive. When you can't cross-subsidise a loss-leader new customer price with inflated renewals further down the line, your acquisition economics change. Some insurers responded by raising new customer prices to meet renewals in the middle rather than dropping renewals to meet new customer prices. That wasn't the intention, but it was a foreseeable consequence. Understanding how insurers calculate your premium helps explain why: pricing models are built around pools of risk, and when you change one input, the whole structure shifts.

To be fair to the FCA, they anticipated some of this. Their guidance acknowledged that the goal wasn't to freeze prices, but to remove the specific distortion caused by loyalty penalties. Most of the time, that's what happened.

What the Industry Got Wrong in Its Predictions

Before the ban took effect, there was a loud chorus from insurers and trade bodies warning of market chaos. Premiums would spike. Insurers would exit the market. Small providers couldn't absorb the systems cost. I heard versions of this from underwriters at two different events in late 2021, and I'll be honest, I thought some of it was lobbying noise, but some of it sounded technically credible. The reality in 2022 was more measured. For more on this, see How the FCA's 2020 Price Walking Ban Changed Insurance Comparisons Forever.

The market didn't collapse. Major providers adapted. And the consumer response was interesting: Citizens Advice had previously estimated that around 11 million people in the UK were in what it called the "loyalty penalty trap" across five markets including home and car insurance. Post-ban, the number of complaints about renewal pricing to the Financial Ombudsman shifted noticeably in composition: fewer loyalty penalty complaints, more complaints about mid-term adjustment charges and add-on transparency. The problem didn't disappear. It moved.

Okay, real talk for a second, and this is slightly embarrassing to admit: I tested the new rules myself by letting my own car insurance auto-renew in February 2022 without shopping around, just to see what happened. First time in about nine years I'd done that. My renewal came in £43 lower than the prior year, and I genuinely wasn't sure if that was the new rules working or just market conditions. My friend tried a similar thing with his contents insurance and got a £12 saving, which he described as "aggressively unimpressive." I'm not 100% sure this works equally well across all providers and policy types, but the direction of travel was real.

Why This Matters Beyond the Price Tag

The price walking ban wasn't just about saving money on premiums, though that matters enormously for people on fixed incomes and tight budgets. It was a statement about what kind of relationship insurers are supposed to have with their customers. The entire premise of price walking was that loyalty should be punished because loyal customers are less likely to leave. That's a predatory logic, and the FCA was right to call it out.

This connects directly to the broader consumer protection shift that defined 2022 in UK insurance regulation. The FCA's new Consumer Duty rules, which were consulted on and finalised through 2022, set a much higher bar for how firms must treat customers overall. Price walking was one symptom of a deeper problem: an industry that had organised itself around extracting value from inertia rather than earning retention through good products and service. The Consumer Duty was the structural answer to that. The price walking ban was the immediate, tangible intervention. For more on this, see The FCA's New Consumer Duty: What It Actually Meant for Insurance Buyers in 2022.

And the ripple effects matter too. When loyal customers stopped being systematically overcharged, the signal to the rest of the market shifted. Comparison sites, which had already built enormous audiences partly by exploiting the gap between new and renewal pricing, had to rethink parts of their model. Some of those dynamics are still playing out. The situation in markets where climate risk is concentrating, like California's home insurance crisis, shows what happens when pricing distortions pile up over years without correction. If you want a sense of where the UK could head without ongoing regulatory discipline, California's FAIR Plan situation is a useful, if alarming, reference point.

The 2022 price walking ban was imperfect. It raised some new customer prices. It didn't fix add-on transparency. It didn't address the complexity of multi-channel pricing in any satisfying way. But it ended a specific, well-documented harm that had cost UK consumers over a billion pounds a year. That's not nothing. That's actually quite a lot.

If your home or motor insurance is up for renewal right now, pull your last three renewal letters and look at the trend. Under the new rules, your insurer cannot quote you more than a new customer would pay through the same channel. If your renewal looks higher than what's showing on comparison sites for equivalent cover, raise it with your insurer directly and cite the FCA's January 2022 pricing rules. They know the rules. Most will adjust rather than lose you. And if they don't, leave. That's exactly what this regulation was designed to make possible.
